Graduate Group 7: Find Your House

Joshua Liu, Yuxiang Wan, Trista Lu, Arun Shrestha

Project-long Course Project as part of COSI 116A: Information Visualization, taught by Prof. Dylan Cashman, Brandeis University.

Motivation

Our project is to provide basic guidance and assist recent graduates in making informed decisions about housing in the Boston area. The way we address the need is to present the comparison of the average minimum wage and average house prices in the Boston area, along with the visual representation of the landscape.

Visualization explanation

We decided to prioritize addressing the housing needs of new graduates in the Boston region through three visualizations. The first visualization employs an X-axis to compare average house and apartment rents in areas like Waltham, Newton, and Watertown, aiding users in quickly identifying affordable options for comparison. The second visualization adds multiple views along with brushing and linking. It features a map with outlined neighborhoods, using colors to indicate relative price differences. Clicking on a neighborhood reveals detailed information for comparison, and a slider tracks post-COVID market trends. So, we have a view for all the neighborhoods and a view for the selections for comparison. However, if the slider for COVID trends becomes a bit infeasible to implement in d3, we might not pursue it as it is a secondary task in our lists. The third visualization also contains brushing and linking. It combines a shaded map reflecting housing prices with a table ranking areas by average wage similar to visualization 2. Users can interact by clicking on the table to highlight corresponding areas on the map, providing a comprehensive view that considers both housing affordability and wage levels. The primary goal is to make it easy for users to discern which areas/neighborhoods are more cost-effective, offering a valuable comparison tool. So, by interacting with the table, they can focus on a subset of areas/neighborhoods. In summary, we wanted to focus on making the tasks exploratory for the users so that they can compare and discover for themselves rather than make it a present task.

Data Analysis

Boston Area House Prices:

We utilized a JSON dataset detailing average house prices across various Boston area cities. This data was processed to create a descending order bar chart, offering a clear visualization of property values across different locations.

Geographic Map of Boston Area:

The map, designed in Adobe XD and saved as an SVG file, visually represents the Boston area. It serves as a foundational element for contextualizing the spatial distribution of our analysis.

Income Group Distribution:

Data on the number of people in different income groups within Boston was stored in a CSV file. Basic preprocessing steps like header removal and trimming were applied to ensure data cleanliness and usability for analysis.

Design Process

We streamlined our bar chart by using average prices for houses and apartments, eliminating the variability caused by different apartment types. This simplification, along with sorting the data in descending order, allows users to instantly recognize the areas with the highest and lowest living costs, facilitating quick and informed decisions.

The final map provides a clear view of income distribution, enhancing users' understanding of each neighborhood's affordability. An interactive feature enables comparisons between areas, helping users to make better-informed decisions about where to live based on comprehensive economic data.

Conclusion

In summary, our project successfully delivered a descriptive bar chart and an interactive map, enabling users to compare average property prices and income brackets across different areas in Boston. These tools provide valuable insights for potential residents evaluating the affordability of various neighborhoods. For future enhancements, we envision introducing a broader color palette to the map to represent data more distinctively. Enriching our dataset with additional parameters could provide deeper analytical capabilities. Furthermore, creating interactive links between the bar chart and map could offer users a more integrated experience, allowing for a seamless transition between different data representations. These improvements would significantly augment the user's ability to make well-informed housing decisions in the Boston area.
